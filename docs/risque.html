Préférences pour le risque¶

De manière générale, les gens n’aiment pas le risque

Pour s’en convaincre, imaginez vous faire offrir:

  • un revenu certain à la retraite de: 50 000/an

  • Un revenu incertain à la retraite (dès suite de placement)

    placement

    50% chance 10 000/an

    50% chance 90 000/an

  • Revenu espéré est de 50 000 dans les deux cas, lequel choisissez-vous?

Rendement et risque¶

Imaginez que le placement est maintenant

placement

50% chance 10 000/an

50% chance 100 000/an

ou encore mieux,

placement

50% chance 10 000/an

50% chance 150 000/an

À un moment, vous allez probablement choisir le placement…

L’approche d’espérance d’utilité (EU)¶

Lottery and expected utility

  • Lottery \(\mathcal L = (p,X \;; 1-p,Y)\) : with probability \(p\) to get \(X\), and probability \(1-p\) to get \(Y\)

  • Expected utility

    \[\mathbf \mathbb{E}_{{ \mathcal L}} (u) = p\times u(X) + (1-p) \times u(Y)\]

Preferences in lotteries

  • Model: Consumer prefers the lottery \(\mathcal L_1\) over the lottery \(\mathcal L_2\) if

    \[\mathbf \mathbb {E}_{{ \mathcal L_1}} (u) > \mathbf \mathbb{E}_{{ \mathcal L_2}} (u)\]

  • Note: Utility is cardinal (units and form matter). This is called the von Neumann and Morgenstern (vNM) utility.

Example

Context

  • \(u(X) = \sqrt{X}\)

  • \(\mathcal L_1 = (0.5,0\;; 0.5,16)\) et \(\mathcal L_2 = (1,6)\)

  • Exercise A: Expected return?

  • Exercise B What does the consumer choose?

  • Exercise C: Does the utility function \(u(X) = X\) result in the same behaviour?

Identifying preferences from choices

Preferences do not vary with affine transformations

  • Affine transformation: \(\widehat u = a u +b\) with \(a>0\)

    \[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \mathbf{E}_{L_1} \widehat u \geq \mathbf{E}_{L_2} \widehat u & \iff a\mathbf{E}_{L_1} u + b \geq a\mathbf{E}_{L_2} u + b \\ & \iff \mathbf{E}_{L_1} u \geq \mathbf{E}_{L_2} u\end{aligned}\end{split}\]

Risk Aversion and Risk Seeking¶

Risk-Averse and Risk-Seeking Consumers¶

Different attitudes towards risk

  • Risk aversion: consider \(L = (p, X\;; 1-p,Y)\)
    If we denote \(Z = p X + (1-p)Y\).

    consumer is risk-averse if he prefers \(\mathcal L' = (1,Z)\) over \(\mathcal L\)

  • Risk-seeking: he prefers \(\mathcal L = (p, X\;; 1-p,Y)\) over \(\mathcal L' = (1,Z)\)

What do we observe?

  • There is some risk-seeking behaviour (casinos … ) but it doesn’t take into account the value of the « experience »

  • A lot of risk aversion (insurance, saving, investment …)

Risk Aversion and Concavity of the Utility¶

Risk aversion

  • Consumer with utility \(u\)

  • Given \((X,Y)\) and \(p\), define \(z \equiv pX + (1-p)Y\)

  • Risk-aversion implies: \(u(Z) \geq pu(X) + (1-p)u(Y)\)

  • This is the equivalent of saying that \(u\) is concave.

Risk-neutrality

  • Indifference between

    \[\mathcal L = (p, X\;; 1-p,Y) \quad and \quad \mathcal L' = (1,Z)\]

  • Corresponds to linear utility \(u(X) = a X + b\), in particular \(u(x) = x\).

Risk Premium¶

Risk Premium¶

Definition

  • Consider the lottery \(\mathcal L = (p,X\;;1-p,Y)\). Define \(Z\) as \(Z \equiv pX+ (1-p)Y\).

  • Find \(Z'\) such that \(u(Z') = pu(X) + (1-p)u(Y)\)

  • \(Z'\) is a certain equivalent of \(\mathcal L\)

  • When risk-averse, \(Z' < Z\) and \(\pi = Z-Z'\) is the risk premium

  • The risk premium is the amount equal to the gain/loss provided by the lottery

  • It’s how much we should give the consumer to entice him to take the risk (compensation). Or, how much he is willing to pay to eliminate of a risk.

  • This is used to evaluate risky assets and demand for insurance.

Example Exercise E: An agent has the utility function \(u(X)=\ln X\). His initial wealth is \(X_0 = 100\) and he’s facing the risk of losing 50 with probability 0.5 and win 50 with probability 0.5. What is the maximum amount he’s willing to pay to avoid that risk?
Exercise F: With \(u(X) = \sqrt X\), is the risk premium smaller?

Applications: Insurance and Investment¶

Several goods we buy are lotteries¶

Financial assets, insurance, investment goods … almost everything

  • Stocks (yields 10 if the firm performs well, yields 0 if she underperforms)

  • Fire insurance (yields 5 if house burns down, yields 0 otherwise)

  • Used car (value of 3 if good quality, value of 0 if lemon)

  • Starting a business (yields 15 if it works , -5 if it fails)

  • Getting married, choosing a career, getting surgery, driving a vehicle… all of these are choices in a risky context.

Insurance and Co-insurance – I¶

Context: two consumers facing risk

  • Each is employed with a 50% probability, income 100

  • Unemployed (U) with a 50% probability, income 0

Employment insurance

  • An insurance program where instead of the consumer 1 getting \(I_1\) and the consumer 2 getting \(I_2\) they both get \((I_1+I_2)/2\) regardless of their employment status

Insurance and Co-insurance – II¶

Insurance is beneficial

  • Without insurance: 50% of getting 0, 50% of getting 100

  • expected utility \(.5 [u(0) + u(100)]\)

  • With insurance 25 % of getting 0, 25% of getting 50, 25% of getting 50, 25% of getting 100

  • expected utility \(.25[u(0) + u(50) + u(50) + u(100)]\)

  • Insurance is beneficial if and only if

    \[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} & .25[u(0) + u(50) + u(50) + u(100)] > .5 [u(0) + u(100)]\\ \iff& u(50) > .5[u(0)+u(100)]\end{aligned}\end{split}\]

  • True if they are risk-averse

Insurance and Co-insurance – III¶

In practice

  • The issue of doing it informally: ex-ante (before knowing the outcome) we want insurance (sharing), but ex-post, if employed, we don’t want to share our income

  • That is where insurance companies come in (making sure people pay even though they got lucky)

The Law of Large Numbers and Insurance¶

Law of large numbers

  • Consider a random variable \(Z\) equal to \(X\) with probability \(p\) and \(Y\) with probability \(1-p\)

  • If \(Z_1, \cdots , Z_n\) are independent with the same distribution \((p,X \;; 1-p,Y)\) Then

    \[si\; N \to +\infty,\quad \frac{1}{N} (Z_1 + Z_2 + \cdots + Z_n) \to pX + (1-p)Y\]

  • The empirical average converges towards the expected value

The Law of Large Numbers and Insurance¶

What this means for insurance

  • When several individuals are sharing risk, and their risks are independent, then everyone recieves exactly their expected income

  • If individuals are risk-averse, this result is good

  • The larger the number of indivuals sharing risk is, the higher their well-being will be
